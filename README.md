# Animalwellfare# US Farm Animal Welfare: Regulations, Gaps, and Reform Proposals

**The United States has no comprehensive federal law protecting farm animals during their lives on farms.** Approximately **10 billion land animals** are slaughtered annually under a patchwork of narrow, outdated federal statutes — most of which explicitly exclude poultry (98%+ of all slaughtered animals) and none of which regulate on-farm living conditions like space, enrichment, or outdoor access. The EU banned battery cages in 2012 and restricted gestation crates in 2013; the US has taken neither step at the federal level. This report provides the complete regulatory landscape, identifies specific gaps with CFR citations, proposes concrete reform standards, and quantifies expected outcomes — structured for direct adaptation into a bilingual GitHub repository document.

## 1. Mevcut Kurallar / Current Regulations

### Federal laws leave most farm animals unprotected

**Animal Welfare Act (AWA) — 7 U.S.C. §§2131–2160; 9 CFR Chapter 1, Subchapter A, Parts 1–4**

The AWA is the only broad federal animal protection statute in the US,  yet it **explicitly excludes farm animals**.  Under **7 U.S.C. §2132(g)**, the definition of “animal” covers dogs, cats, nonhuman primates, guinea pigs, hamsters, rabbits, and other warm-blooded animals used in research, exhibition, or as pets  — but carves out “farm animals, such as, but not limited to, livestock or poultry, used or intended for use as food or fiber.” This exclusion, established by the 1970 amendments (P.L. 91-579), means the ~10 billion land animals raised and slaughtered annually for food have **zero federal on-farm welfare protections**. The AWA is enforced by USDA APHIS Animal Care,  but that enforcement extends only to research facilities, exhibitors, and pet dealers — not a single feedlot, hog barn, or broiler house.

**Humane Methods of Slaughter Act (HMSA) — 7 U.S.C. §§1901–1907; 9 CFR Part 313**

The HMSA requires that covered animals be “rendered insensible to pain” before being “shackled, hoisted, thrown, cast, or cut” (§1902).  It mandates stunning via captive bolt, gunshot, electrical current, or CO₂ gas for cattle, calves, horses, mules, sheep, swine, and goats.  USDA FSIS enforces it through in-plant inspectors  under Directive 6900.2, with authority to halt slaughter lines for violations.   However, the HMSA contains **two critical exclusions**: poultry is not covered (the USDA confirmed this in a 2005 Federal Register Notice  at 70 Fed. Reg. 56,624), and ritual slaughter (kosher, halal) is exempt under §1906.  The Secretary of Agriculture technically could add poultry to “other livestock” under §1904 but has consistently declined (*Levine v. Conner*, 540 F.Supp.2d  1113, N.D. Cal. 2008).  Specific humane stunning methods are codified at 9 CFR §§313.5 (chemical/CO₂), 313.15 (captive bolt), 313.16 (gunshot), and 313.30 (electrical). 

**USDA/FSIS Slaughter and Inspection Regulations — 9 CFR Parts 313 and 381**

For livestock, 9 CFR Part 313 sets detailed requirements: electric prods limited to ≤50V AC (§313.2), access to water required, disabled animals must not be dragged while conscious,  and inspectors can “tag” noncompliant equipment under §313.50.  For poultry, 9 CFR Part 381 governs under the Poultry Products Inspection Act (PPIA, 21 U.S.C. §§451 et seq.),  requiring only “Good Commercial Practices” (GCP)  — an industry-defined standard with no stunning mandate  and no minimum electrical parameters. FSIS can issue Noncompliance Records under Directive 6110.1 but has no authority to require unconsciousness before shackling. A concerning 2025 development: FSIS proposed raising maximum poultry line speeds from **140 to 175 birds per minute** (Docket FSIS-2025-0012), which would worsen both worker safety and animal welfare. 

**FDA Regulations on Antibiotics and Growth Hormones — 21 CFR Part 558**

The FDA addressed antibiotic overuse through three phases. **Guidance for Industry #213** (2013, implemented January 2017) made medically important antibiotics illegal for growth promotion and required veterinary oversight via the Veterinary Feed Directive (21 CFR §558.6).  **GFI #263** (finalized June 2021, effective June 2023) transitioned all remaining OTC medically important antibiotics to prescription status.  In **February 2026**, FDA finalized guidance defining durations of use for medically important antimicrobials in food animals.  Despite these steps, US antimicrobial sales for livestock totaled **6.25 million kg** in 2022, up 4.3% year-over-year,  and the US still permits non-medically-important antibiotics (ionophores like monensin) without veterinary oversight.  On growth hormones: **rBGH/rBST** remains FDA-approved for dairy cattle (though market forces reduced use to ~9.7% of operations by 2014);  steroid implants are widely legal for beef cattle; and **ractopamine** (21 CFR §558.500) is approved for swine, cattle, and turkeys despite being banned in **160+ countries** including the EU, China, and Russia. 

**Twenty-Eight Hour Law — 49 U.S.C. §80502; 9 CFR Part 89**

This 1873-era statute limits animal transport confinement to **28 consecutive hours**  (extendable to 36 by written request)  before mandatory unloading for 5 hours of food, water, and rest. Animals are exempt if the vehicle itself provides food, water, space, and rest opportunity.   Penalties are strikingly weak: **$100–$500** per knowing/willful violation, unchanged since enactment.  Poultry coverage remains legally ambiguous — the statute uses “animals” without definition, and the implementing regulations at 9 CFR Part 89 are titled “Interstate Transportation of Animals (Including Poultry),”   but USDA has taken no definitive position. Enforcement is virtually nonexistent: between 2006 and 2023, USDA conducted only **19 investigations**,  resulting in just **4 official warnings**  and **1 DOJ referral**.

### State-level laws create a patchwork of uneven protection

**At least 15 states** now have laws restricting extreme farm animal confinement,   but most major production states do not. **California Proposition 12** (2018) is the landmark:  it requires **24 sq ft per breeding pig**, **43 sq ft per veal calf**,  and **1 sq ft per egg-laying hen**  in cage-free housing,  and crucially bans the *sale* of non-compliant products regardless of origin state.   The Supreme Court upheld it  5–4 in *National Pork Producers Council v. Ross* (2023).  **Massachusetts Question 3** (2016) imposes similar requirements with 1.5 sq ft per hen  and was strengthened in 2021 (S. 2603) to mandate enrichments like nest boxes, perches, and dust-bathing areas. Other states with confinement bans include **Florida** (gestation crates, 2002), **Arizona** (2006), **Colorado** (2008/2020), **Michigan** (2009/2019),   **Oregon** (2007/2020), and **Ohio** (gestation crate restrictions effective December 31, 2025).   However, the **EATS Act** (Sen. Roger Marshall, R-KS)  and **Save Our Bacon Act** (H.R. 4673, July 2025) threaten to preempt all state animal welfare laws affecting interstate commerce. In July 2025, the DOJ sued California over Prop 12’s egg provisions. 

**Ag-gag laws** criminalize undercover investigations at agricultural facilities.  States with currently enforceable ag-gag statutes include **Iowa** (which passed four versions;  the 2019 and 2021 laws were upheld by the Eighth Circuit in January 2024),   **Alabama**, **Arkansas**,  **Kentucky** (SB 16, passed April 2024),   **Missouri**,  **Montana**, **North Dakota**,  and **South Dakota** (SB 114, 2025).  Courts have struck down ag-gag laws in **Idaho**  (2015, First Amendment), **Utah** (2017), **Wyoming** (2018),  and **Kansas** (Tenth Circuit, 2021).   The Eighth Circuit’s 2024 Iowa ruling created a **circuit split** that may eventually require Supreme Court resolution. 

### Voluntary industry standards range from meaningful to “humanewashing”

The **North American Meat Institute (NAMI)** publishes handling guidelines developed with Dr. Temple Grandin  using HACCP-based audits targeting ≥95% single-shot stunning effectiveness.  The **National Chicken Council (NCC)** issues broiler welfare guidelines covering stocking density, ammonia limits (≤25 ppm), and catching procedures,   but these are industry self-regulatory standards with confidential audit reports. Among third-party certifications, **Animal Welfare Approved (AWA)** from A Greener World is considered the most rigorous, requiring outdoor/pasture access and limiting certification to independent family farms.   **Certified Humane (HFAC)** prohibits cages and crates but does not require outdoor access at its base level.   **Global Animal Partnership (GAP)** uses a 5-Step system from basic (Step 1, no cages/crates) to animal-centered pasture farming (Step 5+).  The ASPCA endorses only **AWA, Certified Humane, and GAP Steps 2+**.  **American Humane Certified** — despite certifying over 1 billion animals — is widely criticized as the weakest major program: it allows farrowing crates, requires no outdoor access, and certifies farms meeting only 85% of criteria.

## 2. Eksikler ve Eleştiri / Gaps and criticisms

### Over 98% of slaughtered land animals lack humane slaughter protection

The single largest gap in US animal welfare law is the **poultry exclusion from the HMSA**. USDA 2024 data shows **9.46 billion broilers**, **198 million turkeys**, and **24.8 million ducks** slaughtered under federal inspection  — totaling **~9.8 billion birds** with zero federal humane slaughter requirements. By contrast, HMSA-covered livestock totaled ~164 million head (31.8 million cattle, 130 million hogs, 2.2 million sheep/lambs, 211,900 calves).  This means **over 98% of all slaughtered land animals** fall outside the HMSA’s protection. The AWA’s farm animal exclusion compounds this: there is no federal law governing how any of these animals are housed, fed, handled, or cared for during their lives. 

The absence of federal on-farm standards means there are **no federal regulations** on minimum cage/pen space, environmental enrichment, outdoor access, maximum stocking density, or genetic selection practices for any farmed species. **Gestation crates** — metal enclosures measuring approximately **2 ft × 6.6 ft** (0.6m × 2.0m) — confine breeding sows so tightly they cannot turn around for nearly their entire 16-week pregnancies.  Over **50% of the 6 million US breeding sows** remain in these crates,  legal in ~40 states. **Battery cages** provide laying hens approximately **67 square inches per bird** — less than a standard sheet of paper  — and still confine roughly **60% of 300–380 million US laying hens**. Both practices are federally legal.

### Live-shackle slaughter and the USDA’s structural conflict

The dominant poultry slaughter method in the US (~95%+ of commercial birds) is **live-shackle processing**: fully conscious birds are grabbed by their legs and hung upside down in metal shackles on a moving line, dragged through an electrified water bath (designed to immobilize rather than guarantee unconsciousness), cut by an automated blade, and passed through a scalding tank. Birds that raise their heads miss the stunner; those that miss the blade enter the scalder alive. There are **no federal minimum electrical parameters** for poultry stunning in the US, unlike EU Regulation 1099/2009 which specifies precise current requirements.

The **USDA’s dual mandate** creates a structural conflict of interest: it simultaneously promotes agricultural industry growth and is supposed to regulate animal welfare.  USDA enforcement actions against licensed facilities **dropped 90% between 2015 and 2020**.  The agency’s FY 2022–2026 strategic plan contains no mention of animal welfare.  When the AWA was enacted in 1966, USDA itself asked Congress not to give it regulatory responsibility due to this conflict.  Academic analysts describe this as textbook **“regulatory capture”** — a regulator serving the industry it oversees. 

### The EU outpaces the US by a decade or more on every major welfare measure

The contrast with EU regulation is stark across every dimension:

- **Battery cages**: EU banned them in 2012 (Directive 1999/74/EC);   enriched colony cages must provide **750 cm² (116 sq in) per hen** with mandatory nest boxes, perches, and litter  — **73% more space** than the US industry norm of 67 sq in. The US has no federal cage ban.
- **Gestation crates**: EU restricted them in 2013   (Directive 2008/120/EC)  to only the first 4 weeks of pregnancy and last week before farrowing, with group housing requiring **2.25 m² (24.2 sq ft) per sow**.  The US has no federal restriction.
- **Poultry slaughter**: EU Regulation 1099/2009 covers all species including poultry, specifying approved stunning methods and minimum electrical parameters. The US HMSA excludes poultry entirely. 
- **Broiler welfare**: EU Directive 2007/43/EC caps stocking density at **33–42 kg/m²** with mandatory lighting, ventilation, mortality monitoring, and personnel certification.  The US has no federal stocking density limit.
- **Growth hormones**: EU banned all growth hormones in cattle via Directive 96/22/EC (1996).  The US permits them widely.
- **Antibiotics**: EU banned all growth-promotion antibiotics in 2006   (Regulation 1831/2003)  — **11 years before** the US’s partial ban in 2017, which still permits non-medically-important antibiotics. 
- **Transport**: EU Regulation (EC) No 1/2005 limits transport to **8 hours standard** (24 hours maximum with vehicle requirements) for all vertebrates including poultry. The US allows **28 hours** and poultry coverage is uncertain.

The EU’s **End the Cage Age** European Citizens’ Initiative (1.4 million signatures)  prompted a Commission commitment to phase out all cages; legislative proposals are expected in 2026   after public consultation gathering 200,000+ responses in late 2025.

### Slaughter, genetics, and systemic reforms

**Extend HMSA to poultry.** The Secretary of Agriculture has authority under §1904 to include poultry as “other livestock” without new legislation, though USDA has declined.  Mercy for Animals has petitioned for inclusion.  Congressional action remains the most certain path. 

**Ban live-shackle slaughter; mandate Controlled Atmosphere Stunning (CAS).** CAS renders birds unconscious through gas mixtures (nitrogen, argon, CO₂) while still in transport containers, eliminating live shackling entirely.  It produces fewer broken bones, less bruising, better meat quality, and improved worker conditions.  CAS accounts for **71% of poultry throughput in the UK** and ~20% in the EU overall. McDonald’s, General Mills, and GAP have committed to CAS sourcing.  Major system manufacturers (Meyn, Marel) make adoption technically feasible at scale.

**Reform broiler genetics.** Modern broilers reach slaughter weight in **35–49 days**  — growth rates increased **400%+ since 1957**, with 85–90% attributable to genetic selection.  The consequences are severe: **27.6% of birds** show poor locomotion at 40 days,  cardiovascular failure kills millions in their sheds, and mortality runs at **1% per week** versus 0.25% for slower-growing breeds.  EFSA’s 2023 expert panel concluded that genetic composition causes more welfare harm than any other factor.  The **Better Chicken Commitment** requires breeds growing at maximum 50g/day (versus 100g/day for conventional), approved by GAP or RSPCA, with 56+ day growing periods.

**Federal legislative proposals.** The **Farm System Reform Act** (Sen. Cory Booker, S.271/H.R.797, reintroduced 2023) proposes a CAFO construction moratorium, complete phase-out of large CAFOs by 2040,  **$10 billion/year** transition funding for farmers,  debt forgiveness, restored country-of-origin labeling, and transfer of pollution liability from contract farmers to corporate integrators.  The act has not passed. Meanwhile, the **EATS Act** threatens to nullify existing state protections by preempting state agricultural welfare laws  — though it faces bipartisan opposition (14 House Republicans and 28 Senate Democrats wrote against it). 

Additional proposed reforms include mandatory veterinary care standards, antibiotic stewardship aligned with the EU’s 2006 ban on all growth-promotion uses,  transport time limits reduced from 28 to 8–12 hours with mandatory poultry coverage, independent welfare inspection authority separate from USDA’s industry-promotion mandate,  and federal whistleblower protections that would repeal or preempt state ag-gag laws.

## 4. Beklenen Sonuçlar / Expected outcomes

### Stress reduction in animals directly improves meat quality

Scientific evidence directly links welfare conditions to measurable stress hormones in meat. Animals slaughtered on-farm show **20× less cortisol** in blood compared to abattoir-slaughtered animals (Sprügel & Peschke, 2023).  Cattle slaughtered without stunning show **~16× higher cortisol** than stunned animals.  High cortisol depletes glycogen reserves,  producing DFD (Dark, Firm, Dry) or PSE (Pale, Soft, Exudative) meat with reduced tenderness, color defects, and shorter shelf life. A 2020 study found **44.3% of cattle** showed high cortisol levels (74.7 ng/mL) indicating poor pre-slaughter handling.  The practical implication is unambiguous: higher welfare → lower pre-slaughter stress → measurably lower cortisol → better meat color, pH, tenderness, water-holding capacity, and shelf life.

### Antibiotic resistance is the strongest public health argument for reform

Approximately **73–80% of all medically important antibiotics** sold in the US go to livestock,  with **84% administered to healthy animals** for disease prevention in crowded conditions rather than treatment.  The CDC estimates **2.8+ million antibiotic-resistant infections** and **over 35,000 deaths** annually in the US.  Globally, AMR was associated with **4.95 million deaths in 2019**,  directly causing 1.27 million. Four superbugs common in factory farming — *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, *Campylobacter*, and non-typhoidal *Salmonella* — were linked to **975,000 human deaths** in 2019.  Without intervention, AMR deaths could reach **10 million per year globally by 2050** (WHO),  at an economic cost of **$1.67 trillion annually**.  Sweden’s experience demonstrates that banning agricultural antibiotic misuse added only **a few cents per kilogram** to retail meat prices while dramatically reducing resistant organisms. 

Factory farming also drives **zoonotic pandemic risk**.  75% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic.  The H5N1 bird flu — with a >50% human fatality rate  — was detected in US dairy cattle for the first time in 2024 across multiple states, with the first confirmed cow-to-human transmission.  The 2009 H1N1 pandemic, linked to pig factory farms, killed over 18,000 people globally.   Reducing stocking densities, improving biosecurity, and eliminating prophylactic antibiotic use would materially lower pandemic emergence risk.

### Economic costs are moderate; potential savings are enormous

The cost of adopting EU-level welfare standards is consistently estimated in cents per pound. Prop 12’s gestation crate elimination costs **3–4 cents per pound of pork**.  Transitioning from battery cages to barn or free-range systems adds approximately **$0.12–$0.30 per pound of chicken**.  Higher welfare pork production costs roughly **$0.20–$0.50 per pound** more.  US retail meat prices already run 20–100% lower than EU prices (US chicken at $1.60–$2.50/lb versus EU at $2.25–$6.75/lb; US beef at $4.93–$10.32/lb  versus EU at $5.40–$11.25/lb), suggesting significant room for welfare-driven cost absorption.

Against these modest per-unit costs, the **public health savings** are potentially transformative. AMR currently costs the US **$55–70 billion annually** in healthcare spending.  Factory farming superbug-related economic losses total **$400 billion globally per year**.  A single pandemic prevention — given COVID-19’s multi-trillion-dollar US economic impact — could offset decades of welfare reform investment. Consumer willingness to pay exists: **78% of Americans** report concern about farm animal welfare,  with average willingness-to-pay premiums of **32% for eggs and 48% for chicken breast**  in survey research, though real-market premiums typically run lower (5–15% for most consumers).  In the EU, **89% of consumers** consider new welfare legislation important, with 69% willing to pay more. 

## Conclusion

The US farm animal welfare framework is defined more by its exclusions than its protections. The AWA excludes farm animals.  The HMSA excludes poultry.  The Twenty-Eight Hour Law is effectively unenforced.   No federal statute addresses how animals live — only, for some species, how they die.   The result is that **99% of US farmed animals** live on factory farms  under conditions that would be illegal in the European Union. The science is clear: welfare reform reduces stress hormones in meat, lowers antibiotic resistance risk, and decreases pandemic probability. The economics are workable: per-unit cost increases are measured in cents while public health savings are measured in billions. The EU’s decade-plus head start demonstrates that phasing out battery cages, restricting gestation crates, and covering poultry in slaughter regulations are politically and economically viable.  What the US lacks is not knowledge or technical capacity but political will  — compounded by USDA’s structural conflict of interest,   ag-gag laws that suppress evidence of abuse,   and federal inaction that forces individual states to act alone against well-funded industry opposition.
